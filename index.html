01 · Overview

What is actually being built

Stripped of jargon: a web tool that pulls a hospital's scattered supply-purchasing data into one place, cleans it up, and finds ways to save money on the physical stuff hospitals buy.

The core problem

Hospitals buy an enormous amount of physical stuff — gloves, gowns, wound dressings, syringes, procedural kits, catheters. This is "med-surg" (medical-surgical) supplies, as opposed to drugs/pharmacy. A mid-size hospital system spends tens of millions a year on it.

The problem: nobody has a clear picture of that spend. The data is scattered across systems that don't talk to each other — the EHR/ERP (Epic, Cerner), group-purchasing-org portals (Vizient), supplier invoices, spreadsheets. Different facilities call the same product by different names and buy it in different pack sizes. So a hospital genuinely can't answer a basic question like "how many different surgical gloves are we buying across our 5 facilities, and are we overpaying for some of them?"

≈

The one-line analogy

It's Mint / YNAB for a hospital's supply closet — it aggregates all your "purchases" from disconnected sources, categorizes them automatically, shows you where the money's going, and nudges you toward cheaper equivalent options. Except the stakes are millions of dollars and the "transactions" arrive as messy hospital data instead of clean bank feeds.

What the tool does — five jobs

1 · See the spend

Charts of total supply spend by category, facility, department, supplier and time. "What are we buying, from whom, for how much."

2 · Standardization savings

Detect where the org buys 8 near-identical gloves; calculate the saving from consolidating to one bought in volume — a person reviews and approves.

3 · Benchmark

Whether the hospital pays more or less than peers (anonymized), and whether it's getting the contracted price it's entitled to.

4 · True cost (TCO)

Not just sticker price — the fuller cost including storage/inventory and waste from expired product.

5 · Track the savings

Prove projected savings materialized — before/after reports leadership can show a value-analysis committee.

+ The plumbing

User roles, commenting, task assignment, PDF/Excel export, and HIPAA-grade security throughout.

MVP is

  • A visibility + savings tool for med-surg / non-pharma supplies
  • Fed by files and feeds first (CSV/Excel/EDI, manual upload)
  • Rules-based standardization & savings math
  • Web-based responsive dashboards with exportable reports
  • Role-based, with review/approve workflow on recommendations
  • HIPAA-grade — encryption, audit logs, access controls

MVP is not

  • Live API integration with Epic/Cerner (that's Phase 2)
  • Pharmacy / drug-spend analytics
  • Demand forecasting or buffer/resilience modeling
  • Automated ordering or purchasing-system write-back
  • A native mobile app (start responsive web)
  • AI-driven recommendations (keep rules-based initially)
!

This is a requirements brief, not a finished spec

The source document is a requirements-gathering brief — written to help whoever owns this idea brief a development team (it even offers to turn itself into formal user stories). So "what they're building" is still at the shape-it stage. That is exactly why the file-first MVP scoping matters: it lets the product prove value before anyone commits to the expensive, certification-heavy live integrations.

03 · Step Zero

How the data gets in

The whole product lives or dies here. Everything downstream — dashboards, savings math, benchmarks — is only as good as the cleaned, unified data underneath. "How is the information filled in?" is three questions: where it comes from, how it enters, and how it's cleaned.

1 · What the raw data is

Every purchase is a line item. For example:

2026-03-14 | Riverside | OR-3 | "NITRILE EXAM GLV MD" | cat# 8825-M | Medline | qty 40 | case | $312.00

The tool collects millions of these from wherever they live. That purchase history sits in the hospital's ERP / materials-management system; two supporting datasets ride alongside — GPO contract price lists (what they should pay) and supplier feeds (what was actually shipped/invoiced).

2 · How it physically enters — three tiers

Tier A · MVP

File upload

Nearly every purchasing system exports purchase history to CSV/Excel. A supply-chain user drags the file in — data's in. Zero vendor cooperation, works day one.

Tier B · MVP

Automated feeds

A scheduled flat file (supplier EDI or nightly ERP export) drops into a secure SFTP folder and the tool picks it up unattended. Same mechanism, no human.

Tier C · Phase 2

Live API integration

Connect directly to Epic/Cerner/Vizient with no human in the loop. The "most technically challenging part" — vendor certification, sandboxes, months of setup. Right to defer.

It's not one-and-done

After the first load, data keeps arriving (upload, feed, or eventually live sync). The tool re-runs normalization on new rows and flags anything new or unmatched for a quick human review. The learned mappings accumulate, so manual effort shrinks over time. The one-line version: a supply-chain person exports their purchase history to a spreadsheet and uploads it; the tool's real work is the invisible cleanup that turns thousands of inconsistently-named rows into one comparable dataset.

04 · The Real IP · catalog/service

The normalization resolver

A deterministic, confidence-scored matcher against a curated item master, with a floor that routes uncertain lines to a human queue. This is the hardest, most net-new piece — and where the product's defensibility lives. No LLM in the per-line path.

// catalog/service — the item-resolution authority.
//
// Loads the item master (canonical products — seeded from GS1 GTIN,
// FDA GUDID and the customer's GPO item master) plus the learned alias
// crosswalk into memory, then answers ResolveItems() with ranked
// candidates above a confidence floor. Deterministic: no LLM per line.
//
// Match-signal weights (starting point; tune via observability):
//
//     Exact GTIN (GS1 barcode)         1.00
//     Confirmed alias (prior mapping)  0.98
//     Manufacturer + mfr catalog#      0.92
//     GUDID device identifier          0.90
//     Vendor + vendor catalog#         0.85
//     Normalized description (exact)   0.75
//     Description fuzzy (trigram)      0.55–0.70   scaled by similarity
//     UNSPSC + attributes              0.45        context, not primary
//     Keyword / partial                0.40        review-only
//
//   score ×= extract_confidence (0..1) from the ingest parser
//
// Routing bands:
//     score ≥ 0.85    AUTO        canonical_item_id stamped, straight through
//     0.50 – 0.85     SUGGESTED   top candidate stamped, flagged for review
//     score < 0.50    UNMATCHED   → queue + ranked candidates, nearest_miss
//
// Tie-breaks (first winning):
//   1. confirmed alias           >  fresh fuzzy hit
//   2. structured key (GTIN/mfr#) >  description text
//   3. prefer the canonical already on a GPO contract
//   4. higher historical volume breaks the rest
//
// UOM is normalized to a base unit (1 CS = 10 BX = 1000 EA) so every
// price is per-comparable-unit — the precondition for the savings math.
//
// Every confirmation writes an alias — the crosswalk learns and the next
// identical raw line auto-maps at 0.98. The manual queue shrinks as
// coverage grows.

Every reviewer decision writes an ItemAlias. The next identical raw line — any facility, any month — then auto-maps at 0.98. Auto-match rate climbs, the manual queue shrinks. This closed loop is the moat, and it's why catalog/service is the one net-new neuron worth its 6–9 engineer-weeks.
✓

Where an LLM earns its place

Not per-row (slow, costly, non-reproducible). Its value is the long-tail review queue — free-text descriptions with no catalog number that the rules can't crack. There an LLM proposes a mapping as a suggestion, raising the auto-match rate and shrinking manual work.

▲

Cold-start advantage

The canonical catalog partly exists off-the-shelf — GS1 GTINs, the FDA GUDID device database, manufacturer catalogs, and the customer's own GPO item master. So the "universe" is seeded from real standards rather than hand-built.

06 · Roles & Workflow

Who uses it, and how work moves

Finance, supply-chain and clinical users see different things and act on different things. Role-based access, commenting on recommendations, task assignment, status tracking, and alerting are all first-class.

Sees: full spend detail, item mappings, all recommendations.

Sees: savings/ROI, spend rollups, exports — less item-level minutiae.

Reviews standardization recommendations for clinical acceptability — is switching gloves/dressings safe and equivalent? Approves or rejects on clinical grounds.

Sees: recommendations + clinical context; comments & approvals.
Ad
Administrator
Governance

Manages users, roles and data connections; owns access control and the audit trail. Configures alert thresholds and facility structure.

Sees: everything + user/role management and audit logs.

The collaboration layer

Comment on recommendations

Threaded comments on any standardization recommendation — clinical and finance weigh in before approval. Resolve/re-open semantics.

Assign tasks & track initiatives

Turn an approved recommendation into an initiative with an owner and status (proposed → approved → in-progress → realized), tracked to completion.

Review / approve workflow

Recommendations are proposed, not auto-applied. A reviewer approves or rejects — the same human-in-the-loop pattern that guards every consequential action.

Alerting

Basic alerts on significant price changes or high-variation categories, routed by role — so the right person notices without watching a dashboard.

Export

Any view or report exports to PDF and Excel for committees and leadership — a first-class output, not an afterthought.

Tiered visibility

Roles graduate what's visible and editable — from full item-level detail to summary-only — enforced on every request, sub-resources included.

07 · Security & Compliance

HIPAA-grade, without a clinical app's burden

The brief flags HIPAA and strong data security as essential. The good news: supply-spend data is a lighter compliance surface than a clinical system — and the controls that matter come pre-wired in the platform.

✓

The PHI nuance that lowers cost

Supply-spend data is overwhelmingly supply-chain and financial, not patient health information. This tool touches little-to-no PHI, so compliance is mostly a Business Associate Agreement plus a strong security posture — encryption, audit logs, access controls, de-identified benchmarking — not a full clinical-grade compliance program. Budgeted as hardening within the build, not a separate workstream.

11 · Gap Analysis

Open questions to resolve before build

The engineering shape is clear; these are the decisions — mostly data-sourcing and go-to-market — that materially change scope. Flagged by whether they block the build or the business model.

Q1 Where does benchmark data come from?Business-critical

Anonymized peer benchmarking needs peer data. Options: a data-sharing consortium of customers, licensed syndicated data, or GPO-supplied benchmarks. This is a business-model question, not a coding one — and the benchmarking capability is only as credible as the data behind it. Resolve before promising benchmarking in the MVP.

Q2 Which EHR/ERP is the first live integration?Phase-2 gating

Epic, Oracle Health and Meditech each have distinct certification programs and timelines. The first pilot customer's system dictates which connect/* neuron gets built first — and starts a 3–6 month vendor clock. Pick based on the pilot, not in the abstract.

Q3 Item-master seed & GPO accessBuild-gating

The resolver's cold-start depends on seeding the canonical catalog. GS1/GUDID are public; the customer's GPO item master is the highest-value seed and needs the customer to grant access. Confirm availability early — it materially affects the auto-match rate on day one.

Q4 A pilot customer with real, messy dataBuild-gating

The resolver weights and fuzzy-match thresholds are a defensible starting point, not measured. They can only be tuned against real purchase-history exports. A committed design-partner hospital early is worth more than any amount of synthetic data.

Q5 Hosting, BAA & data residencyCompliance

HIPAA needs a signed BAA with the hosting provider and clarity on where customer data lives. Lightweight given the low-PHI surface, but must be settled before a customer's data lands.

Q6 When do rules-based recs give way to AI?Roadmap

MVP keeps standardization/consolidation logic rules-based (transparent, defensible to a clinical committee). The trigger to introduce AI-driven recommendations — and the trust/audit story around them — is a Phase-2+ decision to make deliberately, not drift into.

12 · Competitive Landscape

Does this already exist? Yes — and the market is crowded

A multi-source review confirms this is an established, actively-consolidating market — not whitespace. All five capabilities ship today across four vendor categories. The real opportunity is narrower and sharper than "no one does this": it's about who is served.

Don't pitch Provisio as a novel capability — every one of the five jobs is a commodity feature offered by incumbents. Pitch it as better-fit for an underserved tier: analytics-grade spend intelligence, priced and packaged for non-acute and smaller facilities, with file-first onboarding that doesn't assume enterprise EHR/ERP integration.

The four categories

1 · GPO in-house analytics dominant

The three big group-purchasing orgs — Vizient, Premier/PINC AI, HealthTrust — run analytics on massive proprietary datasets (Vizient cites $201B+ spend across 3,100+ systems). Their moat is the data. Aimed at large IDNs.

2 · Independent analytics closest match

Curvo (merged with BroadJump, Sept 2025) is GPO-agnostic and normalizes fragmented supplier data into benchmarks — claims to track ~40% of US non-labor spend across 2,500+ facilities. This is essentially the Provisio concept, already at scale.

3 · Value analysis / CDS

symplr (Value Analysis + Spend Analysis) does evidence-based product comparison, standardization and projected-savings tracking across 400k+ SKUs. Directly covers the standardization + savings jobs.

4 · ERP / procurement-adjacent

Workday (big systems) and Veradigm (all sizes) bundle spend analytics into the ERP; GHX adds Lumere analytics + Epic integration. Small-facility procurement tools (MedProcure, SupplyLasso at $75–225/mo, Hybrent) exist but are PO/inventory tools, not deep analytics.

Where the real opening is

!

The non-acute lane is narrower than the brief assumes

The plan's bet is "target smaller / non-acute facilities." Two findings soften that as clean whitespace: incumbents already contest the lane (Veradigm markets to all sizes; Vizient has a Non-Acute product), and adversarial verification refuted the claim of a clean gap at Premier and HealthTrust. And a cluster of startups (MedProcure, SupplyLasso, Hybrent) already targets small facilities.

But those small-facility players are procurement / purchase-order tools, not analytics engines — the benchmarking / standardization / TCO depth lives with the enterprise vendors aimed at big systems.

✓

The defensible wedge

Analytics-grade spend intelligence — visibility, standardization savings, TCO, ROI — packaged and priced for non-acute and smaller hospitals that can't justify a Vizient / Curvo / symplr enterprise contract. The edge isn't features (all commodity now); it's segment focus, price point, and file-first onboarding — enterprise tools assume EHR/ERP integrations that small facilities don't have.

Strategic implications

Reframe the pitch

Not "a tool that doesn't exist" — a right-sized tool for a tier the incumbents under-serve. Lead with fit and price, not novelty.

Benchmarking is the weak leg

Peer benchmarking depends on data network effects the GPOs and Curvo already own — a cold-start problem. Launch on internal visibility + standardization + savings (needs only the customer's own data); make peer benchmarking a Phase-2 feature contingent on data scale.

Onboarding is the moat you can build

A small facility's pain is that enterprise tools are too heavy to adopt. File-first ingestion + a resolver that self-improves is a real, defensible advantage at this tier.

Capability evidence is from vendors' own marketing pages (fine for "does X exist," not independently audited). Scale figures (Curvo's 40%, Vizient's $201B) are self-reported. The market is consolidating fast — Curvo/BroadJump merged Sept 2025; GHX acquired Syft and Lumere — so names and ownership shift. The small-facility startup names had lighter verification than the enterprise players. Actual adoption and pricing among sub-100-bed / non-acute facilities was not established for any vendor.
